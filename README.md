# Story-to-Screen-Emotional-Experience-Analysis
This project investigates whether the emotional expression in scripts and soundtracks of novel-to-screen adaptations strengthens or weakens audience experience.
# Introduction
In the era of IP-based adaptations, maintain emotional consistency between narrative and soundtrack has become crucial for enhancing audience immersion and emotional resonance. Music, as a powerful emotional medium, plays a vital role in shaping and reinforcing the storytelling experience. However, systematically analyzing and quantifying the emotional alignment between narrative text and accompanying music remains a challenging and underexplored area, especially across different modalities such as text, music score, and audio.
This project focus on investigating the emotional alignment between the dialogues and the soundtrack in the context of a widely recognized IP, Game of Thrones. Specifically, I analyze the dialogues of the Lannister family and their iconic theme music, The Rains of Castamere. The research seeks to answer one questions: How soundtrack impact on narrative emotion in IP-based adaptation? 

# Methodology
I selected text–music score and text–audio as the two cross-modal pairs for alignment because they represent distinct levels of modality similarity. Text and music scores are both symbolic systems that convey emotional intent through structured notation, making them well-suited for projection-based comparison in a shared valence-arousal space. In contrast, audio and text differ fundamentally in form, with one relying on semantic language and the other on acoustic expression. This motivated the use of embedding-based alignment for text and audio, enabling the comparison of latent emotional representations beyond surface features. Together, these two alignment paths offer complementary insights into how emotion is encoded and reflected across modalities in narrative media.
1）	Projection-based Alignment
In the projection-based alignment method, we focused on analyzing emotional consistency between narrative text and musical scores. Specifically, we extracted dialogue from the Lannister family in Game of Thrones and used a BERT-based emotion classifier to compute valence and arousal values for each sentence. For the music score, we parsed the official theme piece The Rains of Castamere using the music21 toolkit, from which we extracted structural features such as mode (major/minor), average pitch, and time signature. These features were then heuristically mapped to the valence-arousal space using psychological music emotion mapping theories. By projecting both text and music into the same 2D emotional space, we were able to compute emotional distance using Euclidean metrics and visualize alignment through scatter plots. This method provided an interpretable framework to assess how closely the soundtrack echoes the emotions conveyed in character dialogue.

2）	 Embedding-based Alignment
For the embedding-based alignment, I extracted dialogue lines from the Lannister family in Game of Thrones and encoded them using SentenceTransformer models (all-MiniLM-L6-v2 and paraphrase-MiniLM-L12-v2) for the script part. A pre-trained DistilRoBERTa model (j-hartmann/emotion-english-distilroberta-base) was then used to generate weakly supervised emotion labels, which were used to train a Random Forest classifier for sentence-level emotion prediction. In the audio modality, the MP3 soundtrack was converted to WAV format and segmented into 10-second clips. I extracted MFCC features for each segment and trained a custom LSTM regression model to predict continuous valence-arousal scores. These predictions were visualized over time and converted into discrete emotion labels via a rule-based VA-to-emotion mapping. Finally, we performed cross-modal alignment by comparing text and audio emotion labels using match statistics, bar plots, and a confusion matrix. This end-to-end pipeline enabled us to quantify and visualize emotional consistency across modalities, combining interpretable projection-based techniques with high-dimensional embedding-based analysis.

# Results and Discussion
The projection-based alignment between text and music score revealed strong emotional coherence. Lannister dialogues exhibited low valence and moderate arousal values, which aligned with the musical features of The Rains of Castamere, resulting in a cosine similarity of 0.9190 and an emotional distance of 0.3669 in the valence-arousal space. For audio, the LSTM regression model predicted dynamic valence-arousal trajectories, which were visualized as time series curves. These predictions were mapped to categorical emotion labels and compared with the text-based emotion labels, yielding a cross-modal matching accuracy of 12.50%. A confusion matrix showed frequent mismatches between semantically adjacent emotions such as fear, sadness, and neutral, highlighting the challenge of aligning symbolic language with acoustic emotion signals. Overall, the results suggest that while symbolic-level emotional alignment (text–score) was relatively strong, alignment across modalities with distinct representations (text–audio) remains a complex task.

The results revealed that emotional alignment is highly modality-dependent. For the text–music score pair, projection-based alignment showed strong emotional coherence: Lannister dialogues exhibited low valence and moderate arousal, closely mirroring the musical features of The Rains of Castamere, with a cosine similarity of 0.919 and an emotional distance of 0.367 in the valence-arousal space. In contrast, the text–audio pair proved significantly more challenging. The LSTM-predicted audio emotion labels yielded a cross-modal matching accuracy of only 12.50%, with frequent mismatches between semantically adjacent emotions such as fear, sadness, and neutral — reflecting the fundamental difficulty of aligning symbolic language with acoustic emotional signals.

Overall, these findings suggest that while symbolic-level emotional alignment between script and music score can be effectively quantified, bridging the gap between text and audio remains a complex task due to the representational differences across modalities.
